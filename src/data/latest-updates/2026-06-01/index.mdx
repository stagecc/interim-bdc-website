## NSRR Sleep Harmonization

The National Sleep Research Resource (NSRR) brings together extensive sleep-related datasets collected across multiple cohorts and clinical studies, including polysomnography, actigraphy, and questionnaire-based data representing tens of thousands of participants.

These datasets represent one of the largest publicly available collections of sleep data. NSRR data include both human studies and animal model data. To facilitate cross-study analyses, NSRR has harmonized each study using a defined harmonization strategy  
([https://github.com/nsrr/nsrr\_data\_harmonization](https://github.com/nsrr/nsrr_data_harmonization)).

The harmonized dataset includes commonly used demographic, lifestyle, and behavioral health variables aligned with the NHLBI Trans-Omics for Precision Medicine (TOPMed) program and BDC standards, as well as core polysomnography-derived sleep measures. All harmonized variables were curated by the NSRR team and follow the naming convention nsrr\_\[variablename\].

Key harmonized variables include age, sex, race, body mass index, smoking status, blood pressure, apnea–hypopnea indices, arousal index, total sleep time, and wake after sleep onset, among others.

Study designs include both case-control studies and longitudinal studies with repeated measures over time, enabling a wide range of analytical approaches.

## NSRR Harmonized Data within BDC

BDC is NHLBI's cloud-based data ecosystem for data, tools, applications, and workflows. Data within BDC include a wide range of cardiovascular, pulmonary, blood, genetics, and genomics data.

For the first time, NSRR harmonized data are available on BDC. By making harmonized NSRR datasets accessible within BDC’s secure, cloud-based environment, investigators can (with the appropriate permissions) link sleep data to unique data within BDC, such as to available genetics, imaging, and a variety of longitudinal outcome data. Investigators also can leverage the BDC platform and workflows to analyze data without the need to move or locally manage large volumes of sensitive data.

## Explore NSRR Data and Tools on BDC

NSRR harmonized datasets are one part of a broader ecosystem of data and tools available through BDC, which also hosts resources from the TOPMed program, sickle cell disease studies, and COVID-related research initiatives.

Researchers can explore available NSRR studies, access harmonized variables, and use NSRR-supported analysis tools—such as the Luna pipeline—directly within BDC. To learn more, visit the [Explore Data in BDC page](/use-bdc/explore-data/) or [join the BDC Community](/join-bdc/) to stay informed about new data releases, tools, and upcoming events.

Currently available studies (with more to come\!) include:

* Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA; phs003288)

* Sleep Heart Health Study (phs003637)

* Cleveland Family Study (phs002715)

* Hispanic Community Health Study / Study of Latinos (phs003543)

To view all hosted datasets from NSRR on BDC, refer to:  **[National Sleep Research Resource (NSRR) | Research Community Data | BDC](/about/studies/?program=National+Sleep+Research+Resource+%28NSRR%29)** 

NSRR datasets include a broad range of raw and derived sleep data in future studies, including polysomnography and actigraphy data. The harmonization process began by identifying an initial set of target terms, such as AHI3%, AHI4%, total sleep duration, overall arousal index, and sleep/wake signal quality flags. The process then mapped the major steps in PSG data generation to identify where heterogeneity is commonly introduced across studies. Using both study-level and variable-level metadata, components were categorized as to the most likely to affect cross-study comparability and revisited the initial target terms to determine whether they needed to be refined, expanded, or redefined to adequately capture this variation. The deposited NSRR data were then mapped to these refined target terms, with further review to identify any additional sources of heterogeneity that should be incorporated. Throughout this process, metadata was standardized accompanying each target term, including harmonized definitions, provenance information, version of AASM definitions for respiratory events, describing the source data and any transformations applied, and standardized NSRR tags to support forward-compatible harmonization.

These integrated BDC tools provide a workflow for sleep data processing and analysis, spanning data manipulation (build, validate, describe, transform-edfs, transform-annots), sleep macro-architecture analyses (hypnogram, evaluate-staging, automated-staging), and basic EEG micro-architecture analyses (time-stats, artifacts, spectral, nrem-transients). Collectively, these tools enable users to construct and validate sample lists, transform EDFs and annotations, perform automated and quality-controlled sleep staging, and extract both large-scale sleep metrics and fine-grained EEG features such as power spectra, artifacts, and NREM transients. More tools, including interactive JupyterLab-based viewers, are planned for future inclusion.
